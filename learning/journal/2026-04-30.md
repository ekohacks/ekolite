# 30 April 2026: Writing for Rhythm

> "The teacher is no longer merely the-one-who-teaches, but one who is himself taught in dialogue with the students, who in turn while being taught also teach." Paulo Freire

PR #32 today, Ebuka's 3.B.1 client reactive store. Five review passes between morning and late afternoon. By the end the PR was clean: the reactive store handles `added`, the helper `withId` got the spread order right, the public API exposes the precise type, the exhaustive switch with `assertNever` plus fallthrough does what an exhaustive switch should do. In story terms, the smallest of the 3.B work landed.

That is not what is worth writing down. What is worth writing down is what kept pulling out of me during the review draft cycle.

## The pull, version one

First draft of the second pass review came back with sections, headers, a Worth Resolving / Worth A Sanity Check / Out Of Scope structure. It read like a checklist. Slid into a flowing prose version. Better.

Then I asked myself if it was Freirean. Honestly, no. Most of it was me naming the problem and answering it for him in the same breath. "What if `fields` already has `_id`?" followed by "the spread runs second, so the body wins, that is the wrong way round." That is banking with a casual accent. The verdict gets delivered between two parentheses. The dev is meant to nod, not to think.

Rewrote it as questions that send him back to the code. Stop after the question. Let him work the answer.

## The pull, version two

Sent that version up. Reply: pure inquiry is good, but a working dev needs hints. Pure questions where the educator pretends not to know what the answer is, that is its own kind of disrespect. The Freirean educator does have knowledge. They just hand it over so the learner can work it out, not so they passively receive.

Rewrote a third time. Question first, then a hint that points at where the answer lives without delivering it whole. "Read it slowly and ask which `_id` wins. Spread order matters: whichever side comes second overrides the first. So if a server `added` message ever ships a `_id` inside `fields`, the parameter you carefully passed in is the one that loses." Names the mechanism. Names the consequence. Stops short of "therefore flip the order".

That version landed. Ebuka came back over the next few hours with three commits that systematically closed every item.

## Where the actual lesson came in

The third pass review had three points: the spread test, the exhaustive switch loophole, the alias at the boundary. Posted it. Got a reply that just said "what you trying to prove here?"

That stopped me cold. Sat with it. Read my own comment back. The exhaustive switch point was technically correct: empty case arms satisfy a `never` default, so the switch is doing less than it looks. But the practical risk in this PR is small. The dev is going to fill those arms in 3.B.3, sees them empty, fills them. The safety net is leaky argument was reaching.

Why was it in the post? Because the post had a rhythm of three. Two points felt thin. Three felt right. So I went looking for a third thing and found a risk that is real but narrow. Real but narrow gets the same airtime in a three bullet post as real and pressing. The reader cannot tell. That is writing for shape rather than for what matters.

Owned it. The user agreed: "so there is actually risk?" Yes, narrow. Wrote a follow up that walked through the actual mechanism (case arms versus default arm, what the compiler sees) so the dev had something useful even though I had inflated the original framing. He took option two from the follow up: throw on early arrival, fallthrough on the two cases. The cleaner of the two I named.

## Why this matters

Yesterday's note was about marking seeds as seeds. Today's is about not planting seeds for the wrong reason. A review that runs to three points because three points reads better is a banking review wearing a dialogical hat. The reader is being given quantity dressed as care. Real care chooses the smallest set of things worth saying. If the third item is genuinely smaller, say so. Or do not say it.

Two strong points are stronger than three where one is filler. The filler does not just waste the dev's time. It dilutes the signal on the points that matter. If everything is worth a look, nothing is.

## What I carry forward

1. **Count points after writing, not before.** Do not start with "I will have three". Start with the code, write what is genuinely there, see what you get. If it is two, post two. If it is one, post one.

2. **Banking dressed casual is still banking.** "Spread runs second, body wins, wrong way round" is a verdict in a thin coat. Question first, mechanism second, stop before the verdict. Let the dev draw it.

3. **Pure inquiry can be its own banking.** Pretending the educator does not know the answer is dishonest. The move is to hand over what you know in a way that lets the learner act, not in a way that turns them into your audience. Question, hint, stop.

4. **"What are you trying to prove" is a gift.** When a senior catches you reaching for shape, the right move is to own it and rewrite the framing, not to defend the inflated version. The question stopped me from posting more inflated reviews this week.
