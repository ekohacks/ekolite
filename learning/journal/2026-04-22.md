# 22 April 2026: The Interface that Wasn't Protecting Anything

> "The teacher is no longer merely the one who teaches, but one who is taught in dialogue with the students, who in turn while being taught also teach." Paulo Freire

A short day in story count, a long one in teaching. Two PRs moved and one blog piece came out of it. The real lesson was about my own questions.

## Morning: reviewing PR #26 the first time

Ebuka's Publications PR landed overnight. Story 3.A.1, single class, single test, the 404 path for unknown publications. Matches the story brief clean. The only addition beyond the brief was a `PublicationInterface` declared above the class and `implements` on the class line. One implementer, same shape on the class, restated above it.

Posted a review. Three questions, kept it dialogical:

1. What do you hope `PublicationInterface` is doing for you?
2. Trace how `MongoInterface` is actually used. Where does it stop being decorative and start being load bearing?
3. If you deleted `PublicationInterface` right now, which test would fail?

Felt clean at the time. Genuinely open questions, the codebase gives an answer, no deposit. Pressed send.

## Midday: reviewing PR #28

Gochi picked up the Rule C ticket. One line change in `StubbedProcessRunner.exec`, the old Rule B test retired, the two new tests pulled straight from the PR #24 follow up comment. CI green. Plus a nice bonus: she deleted `hasNext()` from `ConfigurableResponse` since nothing else used it, and leaving it there kept the door open for the lenient pattern to creep back in. Mongo, FileStorage, ScriptRunner are all Rule C now.

Review posted. Flagged the `hasNext` deletion as a design decision worth making explicit, and asked her reasoning so she could say it out loud. The DoD missed the PR description link back to the #24 comment, so I pointed at that as the one thing to fix before approval.

## Afternoon: PR #26 came back

Ebuka had been working. The latest commit reshaped the class from `Publications` with an interface into `PublicationsWrapper` wrapping `StubbedPublication` behind `PublicationInterface`, with a `createNull(mongo, ws)` factory. Same mould as `MongoWrapper` and `WebSocketWrapper`.

Which is when I realised my three questions had not landed the way I'd hoped.

The question "when does an interface earn its keep" can be answered two ways. The honest answer is "when there's a swap between two genuinely different bodies". The tempting answer is "when there are two implementations". Ebuka had reached for the second and gone and made the second side. Wrong branch, but defensible if you read the question in isolation.

My questions, for all their Freirean posture, had let him answer in the abstract. They were prompts to sit with, not experiments to run. And "sit with" for a developer under pressure translates to "go do". So he went and did.

## The long detour of trying to pull him back

Spent a while drafting the next review. First pass tried to name the whole thing at once: the wrapper copies the shape of Mongo and WebSocket but not the reason they have that shape, there's no production factory so 7.B can't wire it up, the inner class is called stubbed but isn't stubbing anything. Three things stacked in one comment, with three questions at the bottom walking back toward the same point.

Read it back and it felt like an audit. A wall of architectural claims for someone who, from his seat, had heard "interface needs a second side" and gone and produced one.

Sat with it longer. Ended up at a smaller move: one question based on something he could verify and let him pull the thread himself. "There's no `PublicationsWrapper.create(...)`. How does 7.B construct this in production?" Followed naturally by "what does the real one do that the stubbed one doesn't?" which is how you arrive at "nothing, the logic is identical".

Then got to the honesty check. Was the question genuine, or did I already know the answer so firmly that the walk was theatre? Answer: I knew the answer, but not so firmly I'd dismiss his. If Ebuka showed me something different between a real and stubbed Publication that I hadn't thought of, the review changes. That's the line between Socratic theatre and real dialogue, and I wanted the real one.

## The realisation about my own first review

Walked through yesterday's three questions again. Found the problem.

Q1 (what do you hope the interface is doing for you) supplied a candidate answer in its second sentence and knocked it down. Rhetorical, not dialogical.

Q2 (where does the interface stop being decorative and start being load bearing) presupposed the conclusion. Leading.

Q3 (if you deleted the interface, which test would fail) was the genuinely empirical one. The test suite answers, not the reviewer. If I'd driven Q3 alone, with the other two as follow ups to what he found, the whole wrapper detour would not have happened. Q3 on the state at commit `cee7790` lands anyone at "no tests fail, interface is decorative, plain class". That's the whole move.

Abstract questions invite action as avoidance. Concrete experiments force the answer into view. I'd written my review knowing the answer and let him answer in the abstract. He duly answered in the abstract by going and doing.

## Reproducing Q3

Made a worktree at `cee7790`. Deleted the interface and the implements clause. Ran `npx vitest run tests/logic/publications.test.ts`. Green. Ran `tsc --noEmit`. No new errors, only the existing `mongo declared but never read` which is scaffolding for 3.A.2.

Empirical, over and done in three commands. That's what I should have led with last round.

## What I want to carry forward

Three patterns, each bigger than the story it came from.

1. **Dialogical questions need something you can verify, not something to reason about abstractly.** "What does this interface do for you" invites a philosophical answer. "Delete it and tell me what breaks" gets a factual one. The second belongs in a review, the first in a conversation over coffee.

2. **The tempting wrong turn is the question answered in isolation.** When you ask someone to sit with a question, they'll produce an answer that is locally consistent. If the question has more than one locally consistent answer, they'll pick the one that fits their current model of the codebase, not the one you meant. The fix is not to ask better abstract questions. It is to ground them in experiments the code has to settle.

3. **Reproduce before publishing.** The blog piece got stronger the moment I actually ran Q3 on the `cee7790` commit and pasted the vitest output. Conceptual arguments are easy to argue against. Empirical outputs are not. Every teaching piece we publish should have a reproducible move in it, not just a claim.

The pair session is tomorrow. If Ebuka runs Q3 on his own file and walks to the plain class himself, the review is done and the blog piece gets a paragraph added. If he doesn't, that's information too, and the conversation goes somewhere else.
